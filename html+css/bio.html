I was born as a much celebrated honeymoon baby. I recall I began learning English at 4 or 5 from my mom who was teaching at a high school. Shortly after that, I got formal education from native teachers in Canada.


Baby Minjung with a lovely smile and a chubby chin, mid 1980's

A funny story. As a pre-school kid, I found upper case alphabets visually prettier than lower cases. Once, I wrote down Michael Jackson's lyrics all in capital letters. I kept doing it until I knew it was not commonly acceptable. Of course, I didn't do so when I typed command lines in GW-Basic. It was my first programming language as well as first computer experience (1991). This early exposure to coding helped me learn languages like C.

At Junior High, I started building my own homepage with HTML and CGI boards. It was endless modem beeps and black Telnet screen that greeted me every night. Then I entered a private boarding school near Seoul in 2000. Computer access was limited there. But soon I became an avid user of an early generation online social network, which was Korea's no. 1, Alexa's no. 3 website. In fact, back in 2000, three out of top 10 sites in the world were Korean. I'm still impressed by the burst of web phenomena I came to observe in that country where the Internet usage was world record high.

As a teenager, I dreamed of a fiction writer. I even had manuscripts. But given the percentile of 99.9% in College SAT (thankfully!), I realized doing a degree would be a better bet than becoming a good novelist. I went to Seoul National.

On campus, I played guitar in school bands. I developed and administered an online community for the university. I was an editor for a student publication. Off campus, I taught English to underprivileged kids. I interned at three IT companies. By the time I graduated, I got an offer from Samsung (again, thankfully). However, as I did "research" for a Bachelor's thesis (this case study later got published in an SSCI journal), I became more geared toward the management disciplines. One thing led to another, I entered into academia.


Me playing at a school performance, 2005

I joined Intelligent Data Semantics Lab in a final year of undergrad. My training during Master's was mostly in design science, especially data modeling (ER, UML, RDB/SQL), knowledge modeling (RDF, OWL, Prolog), and semantic data mining. I took doctoral-level classes and IS research seminars there, and wrote six papers and one book chapter.

Looking back, it comes natural to me that important moments do coincide. Thanks to my parents flexible about schooling I received international education from early - and I could keep communicating with the friends abroad, through the Internet. Growing up, the ups and downs of the web piqued my interest - and I finally got to study about them, in a more serious manner. It was never planned that I went on to grad school - and now I am in a doctoral program, devoted to researching what lies beneath. These chains of memories are still the driving forces that take me forward.
